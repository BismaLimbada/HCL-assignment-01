The Seven Primitive Operators

In medical device design, the Keystroke-Level Model (KLM) predicts if an expert (like a nurse) can respond to an alarm fast enough.

  • K: Pressing a hardware button.
  • B: Pressing a toggle switch.
  • P: Reaching for a specific dial.
  • H: Homing hands between the device and a notepad.
  • D: Manual drawing/plotting on a chart.
  • M: Mental preparation before a critical action.
  • R: Machine calibration time.

Task: Resetting an Over-Infusion Alarm

An expert nurse needs to silence an alarm and reset the volume:

  1. Mental prep to recognize alarm (M)
  2. Reach for the "Silence" button (P)
  3. Press the "Silence" button (K)
  4. Position hand on the numeric dial (P)
  5. Mental prep to remember code (M)
  6. Enter '0' code (K)

Predicted Operator Sequence: M → P → K → P → M → K

Why use KLM for Medical Devices?

KLM ignores the Gulf of Evaluation (interpretation) because it assumes the expert knows the alarm sound. It is used to ensure that the physical Operators required to stop a dangerous situation are as few as possible.
